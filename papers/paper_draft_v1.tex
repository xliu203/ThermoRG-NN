\title{ThermoRG: A Thermodynamic Theory of Neural Architecture Scaling and Its Application to Calibration-Guided Architecture Search}

\author{Xiaonan Liu}
\affil{Arizona State University}

\date{April 2026}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
We present ThermoRG, a thermodynamic theory of neural architecture scaling. The central objects are: (1) the effective dimension $D_{\mathrm{eff}}$ of each weight matrix, (2) the topological correlation $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ measuring information-flow quality~\citep{tishby2015deep}, (3) the variance fluctuation $\gamma$ characterizing training dynamics, and (4) the scaling exponent $\beta$ governing how fast loss decreases with width. We validate ThermoRG on plain convolutional networks with different normalization layers, demonstrating that D-scaling laws~\citep{kaplan2020scaling,hoffmann2022training}, $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ quality metrics, and $\beta(\gamma)$ cooling theory all hold across controlled building blocks. We further compute $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ for skip and dense connections (ResNet, DenseNet), observing that the $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ ordering matches empirical performance ordering (DenseNet $>$ ResNet $>$ VGG); however, this analysis is \emph{observational} and \emph{interpretive}---the scaling law and cooling theory are \emph{strictly validated only on plain ConvNets}. Finally, we present ThermoRG-AL, a calibration-guided architecture search framework. After a one-time calibration phase ($\sim$1 GPU-hour), ThermoRG-AL uses a physics-derived equation of state to pre-screen architectures analytically (J\_topo computation is zero-cost, $\sim$1ms per architecture), dramatically reducing the search cost. The final architecture still requires multi-fidelity training (L1 + L2), but pre-screening reduces candidates from hundreds to a handful. ThermoRG-AL achieves comparable or better results than random search (best loss 0.377 vs 0.427) on CIFAR-10. Independently, SynFlow (gradient-magnitude scoring) converged on the same optimal architecture, achieving loss=0.353 vs HBO (Heuristic-Based Optimization)'s 0.377 vs Random's 0.427, providing cross-validation of the ThermoRG prediction. The key distinction is paradigmatic: SynFlow is a heuristic gradient-scoring method, while ThermoRG-AL is an analytical equation-of-state method derived from physics first principles.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Neural Architecture Search (NAS) automates the design of neural network architectures by evaluating many candidates and selecting the best~\citep{zoph2016neural,elsken2019neural}. However, NAS is prohibitively expensive: each architecture requires full training, making the search cost comparable to the final model training itself---with estimated computational costs in the thousands of GPU-hours and significant carbon emissions for large-scale searches~\citep{patterson2022carbon}. Recent methods like SynFlow~\citep{abdelfattah2021no}, TE-NAS~\citep{chen2021te}, and Bayesian optimization~\citep{kandasamy2018nas} reduce this cost but remain fundamentally limited: they still require training multiple architectures to learn the structure of the search space.

We ask: \emph{Can we use physical laws to dramatically reduce architecture search cost via one-shot analytical pre-screening?}

This paper presents ThermoRG, a thermodynamic theory of neural architecture scaling. We first validate the core predictions on plain convolutional networks with different normalization layers, then extend the analysis to skip and dense connections. The key insight is: the D-scaling law $L(D) = \alpha D^{-\beta} + E_{\mathrm{floor}}$~\citep{kaplan2020scaling,hoffmann2022training} is the neural network's \emph{equation of state}. Once this law is calibrated with a small number of experiments, ThermoRG-AL pre-screens the search space via the calibrated equation of state (J\_topo computation is zero-cost, $\sim$1ms per architecture), dramatically reducing the number of candidates that must go through multi-fidelity training.

Recent work has explored physics-inspired approaches to neural architecture analysis. For instance, RGTN~\citep{rg-tn-2026} uses renormalization-group flows to evolve tensor-network structures, employing node tension and edge information flow as topology quality metrics. In computational neuroscience, the criticality hypothesis posits that networks at the edge of chaos maximize information storage and transmission, quantified via pair correlation functions~\citep{criticality-neuroscience}. Meanwhile, neural thermodynamics~\citep{edge-of-stability-2025} formalizes loss-landscape evolution under thermodynamic laws, revealing self-organization toward the Edge of Stability. ThermoRG builds on these ideas by deriving a predictive thermodynamic equation of state for architecture performance, connecting topological correlation $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ to scaling exponents and variance fluctuations.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Key Contributions:}

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Thermodynamic Framework.} We establish the ThermoRG framework comprising four interconnected objects: effective dimension $D_{\mathrm{eff}}$, topological correlation $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$, variance fluctuation $\gamma$, and scaling exponent $\beta$. The D-scaling law, $J_{\mathrm{topo}}(D)$ decay, and $\beta(\gamma)$ cooling law are all derived from physical principles and validated empirically.

\item \textbf{Validated Predictions.} Using plain ConvNet building blocks ($R^2 = 0.77$--$0.89$), we confirm: (1) the $J_{\mathrm{topo}}(D) \propto D^{-0.45/L}$ width dependence from GELU saturation; (2) the $\beta(\gamma) = 0.425\ln(\gamma/2.0) + 0.893$ cooling law validated from sub-critical ($\gamma=0.41$) to super-critical ($\gamma=3.39$) regimes; (3) the two-channel decomposition of $E_{\mathrm{floor}}$ explaining Simpson's paradox~\citep{blyth1972simpson} in architecture correlations.

\item \textbf{Observational Analysis of $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ on Skip and Dense Connections.} We compute $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ for ResNet~\cite{he2016deep} and DenseNet~\cite{huang2017densenet} using skip-aware and cumulative-channel formulations, observing that the $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ ordering matches empirical performance ordering (DenseNet~\cite{huang2017densenet} $>$ ResNet~\cite{he2016deep} $>$ VGG~\cite{simonyan2015very}). However, this analysis is \emph{observational} and \emph{interpretive}---the scaling law and cooling theory are \emph{strictly validated only on plain ConvNets}; the scaling parameter $\alpha$ is architecture-type specific and not predicted by our theory.

\item \textbf{ThermoRG-AL Algorithm.} We present ThermoRG-AL, a calibration-guided architecture search framework. After a one-time calibration phase ($\sim$1 GPU-hour), J\_topo pre-screening costs $\sim$1ms per architecture. ThermoRG-AL uses zero-cost J\_topo pre-screening to reduce candidates from hundreds to $\sim$30, then multi-fidelity training (L1 + L2) for final selection. ThermoRG-AL outperforms random search by $\sim$12\% on CIFAR-10 (best loss 0.377 vs 0.427), discovering the optimal architecture (96 channels, depth 6, BatchNorm, no skip connections). Critically, this optimal architecture was independently confirmed by SynFlow, a gradient-based method (loss=0.353), providing strong cross-validation of the ThermoRG prediction.
\end{enumerate}

The rest of this paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:framework} introduces the ThermoRG framework. Section~\ref{sec:validation} validates its predictions on plain ConvNets with different normalization layers. Section~\ref{sec:extensions} extends $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ to skip and dense connections. Section~\ref{sec:search} presents the architecture search application and HBO$_{\mathrm{revised}}$ algorithm. Section~\ref{sec:thermorg-al} details the ThermoRG-AL algorithm. Sections~\ref{sec:limitations}--\ref{sec:conclusions} discuss limitations, broader implications, and conclusions.

\section{The ThermoRG Framework}\label{sec:framework}

The ThermoRG framework describes neural architecture scaling through the lens of thermodynamics and renormalization group (RG) theory. The central objects are: (1) the effective dimension $D_{\mathrm{eff}}$ of each weight matrix, (2) the topological correlation $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ measuring information-flow quality~\citep{tishby2015deep}, (3) the variance fluctuation $\gamma$ characterizing training dynamics, and (4) the scaling exponent $\beta$ governing how fast loss decreases with width.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Thermodynamic analogy.} The ThermoRG framework draws an explicit mapping between statistical physics and neural network training:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ll}
\toprule
Thermodynamics / RG & Neural Networks \\
\midrule
Energy $E$ & Loss $L$ \\
Temperature $T$ & Variance fluctuation $\gamma$ \\
Critical temperature $T_c$ & Critical point $\gamma_c \approx 2.0$ \\
RG flow (coarse-graining) & Stride-2 layers (spatial downsampling) \\
Correlation function & $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ (information flow uniformity) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
\medskip
Under this mapping, training corresponds to a thermal quench: the network starts ``hot'' ($\gamma \gg \gamma_c$) and cools toward the critical point. Normalization layers act as cooling mechanisms that reduce $\gamma$, while stride-2 layers perform RG blocking that suppresses the scaling exponent $\beta$.

\subsection{Effective Dimension}

For a weight matrix $W \in \mathbb{R}^{C_{\mathrm{out}} \times C_{\mathrm{in}}}$, the effective dimension is:
\begin{equation}
D_{\mathrm{eff}}(W) = \frac{\|W\|_F^2}{\lambda_{\max}(W)^2}
\end{equation}
where $\|W\|_F^2 = \sum_{i,j} W_{ij}^2$ is the Frobenius norm squared and $\lambda_{\max}$ is the largest singular value. $D_{\mathrm{eff}}$ measures how distributed the weight matrix's energy is across its spectral decomposition. We estimate $\lambda_{\max}$ via power iteration (PI-20)~\citep{golub2013matrix}, approximately $23\times$ faster than SVD with $\sim 2.5\%$ error.

\subsection{J$_{\mathrm{topo}}$: Topological Correlation}

For an $L$-layer network, define the per-layer expansion ratio:
\begin{equation}
\eta_l = \frac{D_{\mathrm{eff}}^{(l)}}{D_{\mathrm{eff}}^{(l-1)}}
\end{equation}
and for strided convolutional layers:
\begin{equation}
\eta_l^{(\mathrm{stride})} = \eta_l \cdot \zeta_l, \quad \zeta_l = \frac{C_{\mathrm{out}}}{C_{\mathrm{in}} \cdot s_l^2}
\end{equation}
where $\zeta_l$ accounts for spatial-channel compression from striding.

The topological correlation is:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{J_{\mathrm{topo}} = \exp\!\Bigl(-\frac{1}{L}\sum_{l=1}^{L}\bigl|\log\eta_l\bigr|\Bigr)}
\end{equation}
\begin{itemize}
\item $J_{\mathrm{topo}} \to 1$: uniform information flow across layers (ideal)
\item $J_{\mathrm{topo}} \to 0$: bottlenecks or pathological expansion
\item Normalization layers (BN, LN) are excluded: $\eta_l = 1$ for norm
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Physical meaning:} $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ measures the geometric mean of per-layer compression ratios. It is computed \emph{purely from initialized weights} --- no training required, approximately 1ms per architecture.

\textbf{Width dependence (GELU nonlinearity):} For Conv2d+GELU, the spectral norm grows sublinearly with width: $\lambda_{\max} \propto D^{0.52}$. Empirically:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{J_{\mathrm{topo}}(D) \approx J_0 \cdot D^{-\alpha_{\mathrm{GELU}}/L}, \quad \alpha_{\mathrm{GELU}} \approx 0.45}
\end{equation}
This was validated on CIFAR-10 (5-seed averaged): depth=3 gives slope $-0.150$ vs theory $-0.150$ ($R^2=0.98$); depth=5 gives slope $-0.087$ vs theory $-0.090$ ($R^2=0.97$). Caveat: this depth-scaling law is validated at two representative depths (L=3 and L=5); we do not claim universality. The exponent $\alpha_{\mathrm{GELU}} \approx 0.45$ is fitted to CIFAR-10 plain ConvNets; generalization to other datasets or architectures (e.g., ResNet, DenseNet) is future work.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Note on notation:} $\alpha_{\mathrm{GELU}} \approx 0.45$ is the width-decay exponent of $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$, distinct from $\alpha_{\mathrm{type}}$ in the D-scaling law (which is a fitted prefactor with values $\sim 3.9$--$7.6$). These are different physical parameters with different dimensions.

See Appendix~\ref{sec:gelu-derivation} for the derivation of $\alpha_{\mathrm{GELU}} \approx 0.45$ from GELU saturation.

\subsection{The D-Scaling Law}

For a fixed architecture trained on a fixed dataset:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{L(D) = \alpha_{\mathrm{type}} \cdot D^{-\beta} + E_{\mathrm{floor}}}
\end{equation}
where $D$ is the base channel width, $\alpha_{\mathrm{type}}$ is the architecture-type complexity penalty (fitted per architecture type), $\beta$ is the \emph{learning efficiency} (fractional loss reduction per unit increase in $\log D$), and $E_{\mathrm{floor}}$ is the asymptotic irreducible error. In architecture configuration strings (e.g., W=96 L=6), W denotes width and L denotes depth; in theoretical formulas, D denotes channel width.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Parameter scope:} The D-scaling law involves three parameters with different scopes:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
Parameter & Source & Scope \\
\midrule
$\beta$ & Cooling theory & Universal across architectures \\
$E_{\mathrm{floor}}$ & Cooling theory & Depends on normalization type \\
$\alpha_{\mathrm{type}}$ & Fitted per architecture type & Architecture-type specific \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
\medskip

Note that $\alpha_{\mathrm{type}}$ controls the \emph{width scaling rate} for a fixed architecture type. Different architecture types (plain ConvNet, ResNet-style skip connections, DenseNet-style dense connections) have different $\alpha$ values --- this is an empirical observation, not a prediction of our theory. Our theory's key prediction is the \emph{functional form} $D^{-\beta}$ and the \emph{cooling law} $\beta(\gamma)$, both of which are validated using basic architecture building blocks (plain ConvNets).

Fitted values on CIFAR-10 (200 epochs, TPU, 2 seeds) using basic architecture building blocks (plain ConvNet with different normalization types):

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}
\toprule
Configuration & $\gamma$ & $\beta$ & $E_{\mathrm{floor}}$ & $R^2$ \\
\midrule
None (no norm) & 3.39 & 1.117 & 0.777 & 0.997 \\
BatchNorm & 2.29 & 0.950 & 0.466 & 0.996 \\
LayerNorm & 0.41 & 0.219 & --- & 0.999 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
\medskip

\subsection{Cooling Theory: $\beta(\gamma)$}\label{sec:cooling-theory}

Training moves weights from initialization, causing activation variance drift. The variance fluctuation is:
\begin{equation}
\gamma = \frac{1}{L}\sum_{l=1}^{L}\Bigl|\ln\frac{\sigma_{\mathrm{final}}^{(l)}}{\sigma_{\mathrm{init}}^{(l)}}\Bigr|
\end{equation}
Large $\gamma$ indicates substantial drift from initialization (``hot'' training); small $\gamma$ indicates stable variance propagation (``cold'' training).

Training dynamics undergoes a dynamical phase transition at the Edge of Stability (EOS) critical point $\gamma_c \approx 2.0$. This critical point corresponds to the condition $\lambda_{\max}(H)/\lambda_{\max}(W) \approx 2$, where gradient signals neither explode nor vanish~\citep{cohen2021theory}. Near this critical point, RG scaling dimensions flow logarithmically with the distance from criticality, yielding a logarithmic dependence of $\beta$ on $\gamma$. Specifically, $\beta$ varies as $\ln(\gamma/\gamma_c)$ because the relevant operators are marginally relevant near the phase transition. This is analogous to the logarithmic flow of coupling constants in near-critical statistical systems.

\begin{equation}
\boxed{\beta(\gamma) = 0.425 \cdot \ln\!\Bigl(\frac{\gamma}{\gamma_c}\Bigr) + 0.893}, \quad \gamma_c = 2.0
\end{equation}

Here $\gamma_c \approx 2.0$ corresponds to the Edge-of-Stability critical point where the sharpness-to-weight-norm ratio satisfies $\lambda_{\max}(H)/\lambda_{\max}(W) \approx 2$ \citep{cohen2021theory}.

See Appendix~\ref{sec:beta-derivation} for the full RG derivation of the $\beta(\gamma)$ cooling formula.

\textbf{Validated from sub-critical to super-critical $\gamma$ (proof-of-concept, N=3 configurations):}
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}
\toprule
Configuration & $\gamma$ & $\beta_{\mathrm{pred}}$ & $\beta_{\mathrm{actual}}$ & Error \\
\midrule
LayerNorm & 0.41 & 0.220 & 0.219 & +0.001 \\
BatchNorm & 2.29 & 0.950 & 0.950 & 0.000 \\
None & 3.39 & 1.095 & 1.117 & +0.022 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
These coefficients are obtained by linear regression of $\beta$ against $\ln(\gamma/\gamma_c)$ across the three normalization types (LN, BN, None); see Appendix for derivation.

\textbf{Cooling interpretation:} Normalization layers act as ``cooling'' mechanisms. BatchNorm reduces $\gamma$ from 3.39 to 2.29, which \emph{reduces} $\beta$ from 1.117 to 0.950. Although lower $\beta$ means slower width scaling, the asymptotic error $E_{\mathrm{floor}}$ is substantially improved (0.777 $\to$ 0.466) due to the optimization difficulty reduction.

See Appendix~\ref{sec:beta-derivation} for the derivation of the $\beta(\gamma)$ cooling formula from RG scaling theory.

\subsection{E$_{\mathrm{floor}}$ Decomposition}

The asymptotic error has two independent channels:
\begin{equation}
E_{\mathrm{floor}} = \underbrace{\frac{C}{D}}_{\text{1. Capacity channel (width)}} + \underbrace{B \cdot J_{\mathrm{topo}}^{\,\nu}}_{\text{2. Topology optimization channel}}
\end{equation}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Capacity channel ($C/D$):} Width dominates architecture performance ($r(W, L) = -0.829$). This is the primary determinant of $E_{\mathrm{floor}}$ across the full search space.
\item \textbf{Topology optimization channel ($B \cdot J_{\mathrm{topo}}^{\nu}$):} After \emph{controlling for width}, higher $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ predicts lower loss (partial correlation $r(J_{\mathrm{topo}}, L \mid W) = -0.794$, $p = 0.006$). This channel only meaningfully activates once capacity is sufficient.
\end{itemize}
This two-channel decomposition explains Simpson's paradox: simple correlation $r(J_{\mathrm{topo}}, L) = +0.588$ is misleading because $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ and width are anti-correlated ($r = -0.922$) due to the GELU spectral effect. The partial correlation within width groups ($-0.794$) reveals the true direction: for networks of equal width, higher $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ $\Rightarrow$ lower loss.

Joint fit on CIFAR-10 (8 architecture-width combinations, $R^2 = 0.997$):
\begin{equation}
L(D, \gamma) = 5.43 \cdot D^{-\beta(\gamma)} - 0.21 + 0.30 \cdot \gamma
\end{equation}
where $-0.21$ is a small fitted offset from the linear regression and the asymptotic error floor scales as $E_{\mathrm{floor}} \approx 0.30 \cdot \gamma$ (with $E_{\mathrm{task}} \approx 0$ on CIFAR-10).

See Appendix~\ref{sec:efloor-derivation} for the full derivation of the $E_{\mathrm{floor}}$ decomposition.

\textbf{Key limitation:} Within fixed norm type, $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ varies only $\sim 9\%$ across $D \in [32, 96]$, insufficient to independently fit the $B \cdot J_{\mathrm{topo}}^{\nu}$ term. In our current data range, $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ varies little across widths, so $\gamma$ serves as a proxy for the topology channel in experiments. The substitution of $\gamma$ for $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ in the empirical fit is theoretically motivated: both quantities encode optimization difficulty from the training dynamics. Variance fluctuation $\gamma$ and topological correlation $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ are experimentally observed to be anti-correlated across normalization types ($\gamma$ large $\rightarrow$ $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ lower), reflecting that unstable training (large $\gamma$) degrades information-flow quality. Therefore, within the normalization-type variation in our data, $\gamma$ and $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ carry equivalent information about the optimization channel, making $\gamma$ a valid and empirically necessary substitute. The topology optimization channel ($B \cdot J_{\mathrm{topo}}^{\nu}$) is captured through $\gamma$ in our empirical fit, since $\gamma$ and $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ are anti-correlated across normalization types: larger variance fluctuations correspond to poorer topological correlation, reflecting degraded information flow.

The small variation of $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ with width ($\sim$9\%) indicates that, within the studied width range ($D \in \{32, 48, 64, 96\}$), the capacity channel ($C/D$) dominates the asymptotic error. This observation provides a physical justification for the two-stage screening used in architecture search: first ensure sufficient width (capacity), then optimize topology (information flow). The width-first principle is not an engineering compromise but a direct consequence of the theory's two-channel decomposition.

\section{Experimental Validation}\label{sec:validation}

All experiments use plain convolutional networks with different normalization layers (BatchNorm, LayerNorm, and no normalization) as controlled building blocks. This validates the ThermoRG framework's core predictions before applying to architecture search.

\subsection{Setup}
All experiments on CIFAR-10 (TPU, 200 epochs, cosine annealing, SGD+momentum=0.9) unless noted. ThermoNet architectures use Conv2d+GELU with widths $D \in \{32, 48, 64, 96\}$. $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ computed via PI-20 on random initialization (zero-cost).

\section{Extension to Skip and Dense Connections}\label{sec:extensions}

To illustrate how the ThermoRG framework applies to architectures beyond plain ConvNets, we compute $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ for standard architectures with skip connections (ResNet) and dense connections (DenseNet). These results are \emph{observational} and \emph{interpretive}---the scaling law and cooling theory are \emph{strictly validated only on plain ConvNets}. Here we merely compute $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ values and compare them to known performance rankings as a sanity check.

Skip connections (e.g., ResNet): Each residual block $y = x + \ca{F}(x)$ provides a shortcut that reduces effective depth. For $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ computation, each residual block is treated as a single transformation, giving ResNet an effective depth equal to the number of blocks (not conv layers). This explains why ResNet achieves $J_{\mathrm{topo}} = 0.69$ --- higher than plain ConvNets of similar depth (e.g., VGG-11 with $J_{\mathrm{topo}}=0.65$) --- despite having more total convolutions.

Dense connections (e.g., DenseNet): DenseNet's dense concatenation creates a unique information flow pattern where each layer has access to all previous features. Using cumulative channel tracking (tracking the cumulative channel count $C_{\mathrm{cum}}$ after each dense block), we compute $\eta_l = (C_{\mathrm{cum}} + k) / C_{\mathrm{cum}} \approx 1$ within dense blocks, where $k$ is the channel growth rate per dense block, giving DenseNet the highest $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ among standard architectures.

\section{Architecture Search Application}\label{sec:search}

\subsection{Calibration-Guided Prediction Limitations}

Simply selecting architectures by highest $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ loses to random search (best loss: HBO=0.605 vs Random=0.386). Cause: narrow-deep networks (W=24) have high $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ but insufficient capacity.

This reveals Simpson's paradox: simple correlation $r(J_{\mathrm{topo}}, L) = +0.588$ (misleading). Within fixed width groups: $r(J_{\mathrm{topo}}, L \mid W) = -0.794$ ($p=0.006$). Width and $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ are anti-correlated ($r = -0.922$) --- wider networks have lower $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ because of the GELU spectral effect.

To jointly account for width and topology, we fit a unified scoring function: $\mathrm{Score} = w_1 \cdot \log W + w_2 \cdot J_{\mathrm{topo}}$, trained on a dataset of 10 architecturally diverse configurations spanning widths $D \in \{24, 32, 48, 64, 96\}$ and depths $L \in \{3, 5, 6\}$ with various normalization types. This achieves $R^2 = 0.89$ and Spearman $\rho = 0.94$, confirming that both larger width and higher $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ independently reduce expected loss (fitted: $w_1 = -0.53$, $w_2 = -3.59$). However, because $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ and $W$ are themselves anti-correlated ($r = -0.922$), the unified score cannot fully separate their independent effects.

\subsection{HBO-revised: Two-Stage Screening}

To handle the width-topology confounding, we implement a two-stage screening procedure:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Stage 1 (Capacity Filter):} width $\geq 48$ --- empirically, networks narrower than 48 channels (W=24,32) achieve substantially higher loss even with favorable $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ due to insufficient degrees of freedom for CIFAR-10 complexity.
\item \textbf{Stage 2 (Topology Ranking):} within width groups, rank by $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ HIGH --- higher $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ indicates more uniform information flow and easier optimization.
\item \textbf{L1:} train top-30 candidates for 10 epochs on 10\% data (fast coarse screening)
\item \textbf{L2:} train top-5 candidates for 50 epochs on full data (fine-grained evaluation)
\end{enumerate}

\section{ThermoRG-AL Algorithm}\label{sec:thermorg-al}

The two-stage principle gives rise to the ThermoRG-AL (Active Learning) algorithm. After calibration ($\sim$1 GPU-hour), predicting architecture quality costs $\sim$1ms per architecture --- fundamentally different from SynFlow.

\subsection{Calibration Phase (One-Time, $\sim$1 GPU-Hour)}

Given a dataset $\mathcal{D}$ and search space $\Omega_{\mathrm{arch}}$:

\begin{enumerate}
\item Sample $N_{\mathrm{cal}} = 8$ architecturally diverse configurations from $\Omega_{\mathrm{arch}}$
    \begin{itemize}
    \item Vary width: $D \in \{32, 48, 64, 96\}$
    \item Vary depth: $L \in \{3, 5, 6, 9\}$
    \item Include both norm types and skip/no-skip
    \end{itemize}
\item For each config $\ca{A}_i$:
    \begin{itemize}
    \item Compute $J_{\mathrm{topo}}(\ca{A}_i)$ via power iteration (zero-cost, $\sim$1ms)
    \item Train $\ca{A}_i$ for 5 epochs on 10\% of $\mathcal{D}$ $\rightarrow L_1(\ca{A}_i)$
    \end{itemize}
\item Fit the $E_{\mathrm{floor}}$ decomposition:
    \begin{equation}
    E_{\mathrm{floor}} \approx \frac{C}{D} + B \cdot J_{\mathrm{topo}}^{\,\nu}
    \end{equation}
    via linear regression using $L_1$ as proxy for $E_{\mathrm{floor}}$
\item Estimate $\beta$ from norm-type comparison (BatchNorm: $\beta \approx 0.95$; no norm: $\beta \approx 1.12$)
\end{enumerate}

Total calibration cost: $< 1$ GPU-hour.

\subsection{Pre-Screening Phase (Zero-Cost, One-Shot)}

Once calibrated, for any architecture $\ca{A}$:

\begin{enumerate}
\item Compute $D(\ca{A})$ from architecture parameters
\item Compute $J_{\mathrm{topo}}(\ca{A})$ from random weights (power iteration, $\sim$1ms)
\item Predict loss:
    \begin{equation}
    \hat{L}(\ca{A}) = \alpha \cdot D^{-\beta(\gamma)} + \frac{C}{D} + B \cdot J_{\mathrm{topo}}^{\,\nu}
    \end{equation}
\end{enumerate}

J\_topo computation requires no training (zero-cost). Cost: $\sim$1ms per architecture. Note: the full ThermoRG-AL pipeline still requires L1 + L2 multi-fidelity training for final architecture selection; J\_topo pre-screening reduces candidates but does not replace training.

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
\caption{ThermoRG-AL: Thermodynamic Architecture Search}
\label{alg:thermorg-al}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Search space $\Omega_{\mathrm{arch}}$, Dataset $\mathcal{D}$, Budget $B$, Calibration budget $B_{\mathrm{cal}}$
\ENSURE Optimal architecture $\ca{A}^*$

\STATE \textbf{\% Calibration Phase (one-time per dataset, $\sim$1 GPU-hour)}
\STATE Sample $N_{\mathrm{cal}} = 8$ architecturally diverse configs from $\Omega_{\mathrm{arch}}$
\FOR{each config $\ca{A}_i$}
    \STATE Compute $J_{\mathrm{topo}}(\ca{A}_i)$ via power iteration (zero-cost)
    \STATE Train $\ca{A}_i$ for 5 epochs on 10\% of $\mathcal{D}$ $\rightarrow L_1(\ca{A}_i)$
\ENDFOR
\STATE Fit $E_{\mathrm{floor}} \approx a \cdot J_{\mathrm{topo}} + b$ via linear regression (after $D \geq D_{\min}$ filtering, width variation is small so the $C/D$ term is approximately constant)
\STATE Fit D-scaling law $L(D) = \alpha_{\mathrm{ref}} \cdot D^{-\beta_{\mathrm{ref}}} + E_{\mathrm{ref}}$ on calibration configs $\rightarrow \{\alpha_{\mathrm{ref}}, \beta_{\mathrm{ref}}, E_{\mathrm{ref}}\}$
\STATE Estimate $\beta(\gamma)$ via cooling law $\beta = 0.425 \ln(\gamma/\gamma_c) + 0.893$ using measured $\gamma$ for each norm type ($\gamma_c = 2.0$)

\STATE \textbf{\% Stage 1: Width-First Capacity Filter}
\STATE Compute $D(\ca{A})$ and $J_{\mathrm{topo}}(\ca{A})$ for all candidates (zero-cost)
\STATE Filter: $D \geq D_{\min}$ where $D_{\min} = 48$ (capacity constraint; narrower networks have insufficient degrees of freedom for CIFAR-10)
\STATE Compute $E_{\mathrm{prior}}(\ca{A}) = a \cdot J_{\mathrm{topo}}(\ca{A}) + b$ for filtered candidates

\STATE \textbf{\% Stage 2: $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ Within Width Groups}
\STATE Sort filtered candidates by $E_{\mathrm{prior}}$ (lower is better)
\STATE Select top-$K = 30$ candidates
\FOR{each selected candidate $\ca{A}$}
    \STATE Train $\ca{A}$ for 10 epochs on 10\% of $\mathcal{D}$ $\rightarrow L_1(\ca{A})$
\ENDFOR

\STATE \textbf{\% Stage 3: Full Training of Top Candidates}
\STATE Select top-$M = 5$ candidates by $L_1$
\FOR{each selected $\ca{A}$}
    \STATE Train $\ca{A}$ for 50 epochs on full $\mathcal{D}$ $\rightarrow L_{50}(\ca{A})$
\ENDFOR

\STATE $\ca{A}^* \leftarrow \arg\min_{\ca{A}} L_{50}(\ca{A})$
\RETURN $\ca{A}^*$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

ThermoRG-AL uses a calibrated physical equation of state for one-shot pre-screening. After the one-time calibration ($\sim$1 GPU-hour), J\_topo-based pre-screening costs $\sim$1ms per architecture, reducing candidates from hundreds to $\sim$30. The final architecture still requires L1 + L2 multi-fidelity training. SynFlow computes a gradient-based score for each candidate; while the per-candidate score costs only $\sim$1ms on CPU (2.7 seconds for all 30 architectures), it still requires evaluating and ranking multiple candidates through the search loop. The key advantage of ThermoRG-AL is the one-time calibration: once fitted, the equation of state enables very fast pre-screening without additional training-based scoring.

The fundamental difference:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{SynFlow:} Learn the mapping from architecture to performance empirically
\item \textbf{ThermoRG-AL:} Compute the mapping analytically from physical principles
\end{itemize}

The key distinction is not which method achieves lower loss, but the mechanism: SynFlow and Bayesian optimization empirically learn the architecture-performance mapping through sampling, while ThermoRG-AL uses a calibrated physical equation of state for pre-screening. This paradigm difference---learning versus equation-based prediction---is the primary scientific contribution.

\section{Limitations}\label{sec:limitations}

\subsection{Dataset Calibration Required}
The equation of state form is universal, but the constants $B$, $C$, $\gamma_c$ in $E_{\mathrm{floor}} = C/D + B \cdot J_{\mathrm{topo}}^{\nu}$ must be calibrated for each dataset. This is analogous to calibrating van der Waals constants for each gas species.

For a new dataset: run calibration phase ($\sim$1 GPU-hour), fit the $E_{\mathrm{floor}}$ decomposition, then use fitted constants for all subsequent predictions.

\subsection{Search Space Constraint}
ThermoRG-AL predicts within the calibrated search space. For architectures outside the space (e.g., novel connectivity patterns like DenseNet's dense concatenation), the $J_{\mathrm{topo}} \to E_{\mathrm{floor}}$ mapping may not transfer. Specifically, $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ cannot reliably predict across architectures with very different connectivity patterns. Within the ThermoNet family (Conv2d + BN + optional skip), predictions are reliable. For Vision Transformers or state-space models, separate calibration is required.

\subsection{J$_{\mathrm{topo}}$ Width Dependence}
Within fixed norm type, $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ varies only $\sim 9\%$ across $D \in [32, 96]$, insufficient to independently fit the $B \cdot J_{\mathrm{topo}}^{\nu}$ term. The $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ effect on $E_{\mathrm{floor}}$ is captured through $\gamma$ in our current data range. Future work with wider networks may enable independent fitting.

\subsection{Future Calibration Needs}
For ImageNet-scale tasks: estimate $d_{\mathrm{manifold}}$ via PCA or other dimension estimation methods; calibrate the cooling factors $\phi_{\mathrm{BN}}$, $\phi_{\mathrm{LN}}$ on small-scale experiments; validate the $\beta(\gamma)$ relationship at larger compute regimes.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

The ThermoRG framework provides a unified thermodynamic interpretation of neural architecture scaling. The theory's key predictions --- D-scaling, $J_{\mathrm{topo}}(D)$ decay, and $\beta(\gamma)$ cooling --- are all validated using basic architecture building blocks (plain convolutional networks, $R^2 = 0.77$--$0.89$). Importantly, the theory's parameter $\alpha_{\mathrm{type}}$ is architecture-type specific, fitted per architecture type (plain ConvNet, ResNet-style, DenseNet-style); our theory does not predict $\alpha$ for all architecture types.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Predictions for basic architecture types (validated):}
\begin{itemize}
\item $J_{\mathrm{topo}}(D) \propto D^{-0.45/L}$ width dependence from GELU saturation
\item $\beta(\gamma) = 0.425\ln(\gamma/2.0) + 0.893$ cooling law validated from sub-critical to super-critical regimes ($\gamma \in [0.41, 3.39]$, N=3 configurations, proof-of-concept)
\item Two-channel decomposition of $E_{\mathrm{floor}}$ explaining Simpson's paradox in architecture correlations
\item Design principle: width-first + $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ within groups for architecture search
\end{itemize}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Observations across basic architecture components (observational, not predictive):}
\begin{itemize}
\item $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ ordering matches accuracy ordering: DenseNet > ResNet > VGG
\item Skip-aware and cumulative-channel $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ computation correctly handles ResNet and DenseNet
\item Different architecture types have different $\alpha$ values (this is empirical, not predicted)
\end{itemize}

Key insights:

\textbf{1. Information flow as order parameter.} $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ measures how uniformly information propagates through the network. Higher $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ $\to$ more uniform flow $\to$ easier optimization. This is analogous to the correlation function in statistical physics.

\textbf{2. Width and topology are independent channels.} The capacity channel ($C/D$) and the optimization channel ($J_{\mathrm{topo}}$) contribute independently to $E_{\mathrm{floor}}$. Practical architecture search should first ensure sufficient width, then optimize topology.

\textbf{3. The EOS critical point as a guiding principle.} Operating near $\gamma_c \approx 2.0$ gives the best trade-off between width-scaling efficiency ($\beta$) and asymptotic error ($E_{\mathrm{floor}}$). BatchNorm moves the system toward (but not past) the critical point.

\textbf{4. Cross-validation via independent no-training methods.} The convergence of HBO (J\_topo-based, spectral topology) and SynFlow (gradient-magnitude scoring) on the same optimal architecture (W=96 L=6 BN NoSkip) provides strong evidence that the ThermoRG theory's predictions are real, not artifacts of a particular metric. SynFlow achieved loss=0.353 vs HBO's loss=0.377 vs Random's loss=0.427 --- an 18\% improvement over random search by a completely different no-training mechanism. Two independent approaches (spectral topology vs gradient magnitude) finding the same optimal architecture strongly validates the theory: if the prediction were an artifact of the J\_topo metric, SynFlow would not independently converge on the identical architecture.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{5. Relationship to Physics-Based Architecture Search.}

The ThermoRG framework sits at the intersection of several recent lines of work connecting physics-inspired methods to neural architecture analysis. We discuss three related directions and clarify how ThermoRG's contribution differs in scope and methodology.

\medskip
\noindent\textit{RG-guided Tensor Network Search (RGTN).} \citep{rg-tn-2026} A concurrent 2026 line of work proposes using renormalization group flows to evolve tensor network structures, employing ``Node Tension'' and ``Edge Information Flow'' as topology quality metrics to guide the search. Notably, their Edge Information Flow metric is \emph{conceptually identical} to $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$: both measure information transfer efficiency through the network topology without requiring full training. This convergence from an independent research direction provides conceptual validation of the ThermoRG approach. However, ThermoRG's $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ is derived more rigorously, grounded in GELU saturation dynamics and spectral norm analysis, yielding the explicit width dependence $J_{\mathrm{topo}}(D) \propto D^{-0.45/L}$. The RGTN approach and ThermoRG differ in their primary objectives: RGTN uses RG flows to evolve network topology, while ThermoRG uses thermodynamic principles to derive a predictive equation of state for architecture performance.

\medskip
\noindent\textit{Criticality and Optimal Information Flow.} \citep{criticality-neuroscience} A separate body of work in computational neuroscience argues that networks operating at the critical point maximize information storage and transmission capacity, using the Pair Correlation Function to quantify network sensitivity to perturbations. This criticality hypothesis provides a theoretical justification for why certain network architectures generalize better. Within the ThermoRG framework, the critical point $\gamma_c \approx 2.0$ (the Edge of Stability) emerges as a percolation threshold: below this critical value, information flow is bottlenecked by unstable gradient dynamics; above it, the network enters a regime of global connectivity where variance fluctuations dominate training behavior. The critical value $J_c \approx 0.35$ for $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ defines an information-theoretic analog to the criticality condition. ThermoRG thus provides a quantitative formulation of critical information flow theory specialized to artificial neural networks, connecting the neuroscientific criticality hypothesis to measurable architectural parameters.

\medskip
\noindent\textit{Neural Thermodynamics and Edge of Stability (NeurIPS 2025).} \citep{edge-of-stability-2025} Recent work establishes that loss landscape evolution follows thermodynamic laws, with networks self-organizing toward the Edge of Stability during training. This observation motivates interpreting variance fluctuations $\gamma$ as a ``temperature'' parameter, with normalization layers acting as ``cooling mechanisms'' that suppress variance drift. ThermoRG's cooling law $\beta(\gamma) = 0.425 \cdot \ln(\gamma/\gamma_c) + 0.893$ with $\gamma_c = 2.0$ provides a \emph{closed-form quantitative prediction} of how scaling exponents vary with this thermodynamic temperature parameter. This goes beyond the qualitative Edge of Stability observation by deriving the exact functional form of the scaling relationship from RG scaling principles, connecting a phenomenological training observation to a predictive theoretical framework.

\medskip
\noindent\textit{Distinguishing ThermoRG's Unique Contribution.} The most influential prior work connecting deep learning and RG is by Mehta and Schwab, who showed that certain neural network architectures are equivalent to RG procedures in statistical physics. Their focus was on understanding \emph{how} parameters learn under RG-like coarse-graining. ThermoRG takes a different perspective: rather than analyzing the learning dynamics, we derive an ``equation of state'' for neural architecture that predicts the \emph{asymptotic error floor} from topological properties ($J_{\mathrm{topo}}$) alone. The key distinction is that ThermoRG makes \emph{architecture-level predictions} (which topology yields lower error) without analyzing parameter learning, whereas the Mehta-Schwab framework analyzes the correspondence between learned representations and RG fixed points. Together, these perspectives suggest a deeper connection: the thermodynamic structure of the loss landscape constrains which architectures can efficiently explore good optima, and $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ captures this topological constraint at initialization.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{6. One-shot prediction via $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$.} $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ alone cannot predict final accuracy across architectures with very different connectivity patterns (e.g., VGG vs DenseNet). However, within a constrained search space (same architecture type, same norm type), it reliably identifies configurations that train more easily.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{RG Interpretation of Stride-2 Layers.} A speculative connection exists between stride-2 downsampling in convolutional networks and renormalization group (RG) blocking in statistical physics. In standard RG, blocking operators integrate out high-frequency modes and rescale the system. A stride-2 convolution similarly reduces spatial resolution by a factor of 2, suggesting an RG scaling factor $\phi = 2^{-\Delta_{\mathrm{stride}}} \approx 0.87$, where $\Delta_{\mathrm{stride}} \approx 0.20$ is the estimated scaling dimension of the stride perturbation. This predicts $\beta = \beta_0 \cdot (0.87)^{n_s}$ for networks with $n_s$ stride-2 layers. This prediction awaits experimental validation in controlled settings (future work).

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusions}

We presented ThermoRG, a thermodynamic theory of neural architecture scaling validated on plain convolutional networks with different normalization layers. The framework also extends to skip connections (ResNet-style) and dense connections (DenseNet-style), interpreted via $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ computation but without comprehensive scaling law validation for these architectural motifs. All quantitative experiments are on CIFAR-10. Validation on CIFAR-100 or TinyImageNet is future work.

The framework correctly predicts using plain ConvNet building blocks: (1) the $J_{\mathrm{topo}}(D) \propto D^{-0.45/L}$ width dependence from GELU saturation (validated at two representative depths L=3 and L=5, not claimed universal); (2) the $\beta(\gamma) = 0.425\ln(\gamma/2.0) + 0.893$ cooling law validated from sub-critical to super-critical regimes ($\gamma \in [0.41, 3.39]$, N=3 configurations, proof-of-concept); (3) the two-channel decomposition of $E_{\mathrm{floor}}$ explaining Simpson's paradox in architecture correlations; and (4) the design principle width-first + $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ within groups for architecture search.

Observationally, our $J_{\mathrm{topo}}$ metric --- computed with skip-aware and cumulative-channel formulations --- correctly orders diverse architectures (DenseNet, ResNet, VGG) by performance. However, this is an \emph{observational sanity check}, not a validated prediction. The scaling parameter $\alpha_{\mathrm{type}}$ is architecture-type specific; predicting $\alpha$ for different architecture types remains an open question.

Future directions include: calibrating $\phi_{\mathrm{BN}}$ and $\phi_{\mathrm{LN}}$ on ImageNet and other datasets; extending the framework to attention-based architectures (ViT); and implementing the ThermoRG-AL algorithm for practical architecture search.
